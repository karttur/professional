Introduction
The Okavango Delta, Botsvana is one of the world's largest inland deltas. The Okavango Delta is partly fed by local summer rains. Most of the water, however, comes from rainfall in the Angolan highlands. It takes the water several months to reach the Pandhandle - a tectonic entry corridor into the Delta. It then takes another two to three months for the water to transverse the Delta. Hence the flooding is at its maximum during winter, the driest part of the year. The water from the Angolan highlands carry approximatey 500 000 tons of material to the Delta each year. This material accumulates in the Delta. Mostly on the beds of the channels, and adjacent areas. The channel beds rise over the surroundings which eventually forces water into new channels. This continously remolds the Okavango Delta. The weight of the material causes local earth quakes. The Delta is also situated on the edge of the African rift valley which causes regional earth quakes. The earth quakes changes the general topography of the Delta. Old shorelines and topographic anomalies indicate that these tectonic processes slightly tilts the Delta towards the Linayanti. This will eventually seal the faith of the Delta by diverting water flow to the Zambezi river.
